What Are Orthogonal Trajectories?

In mathematics, an orthogonal trajectory is a curve that intersects each curve of a given family of curves at right angles (orthogonally).

If one family of curves is given by F(x, y, c) = 0, where c is a constant parameter, then the orthogonal trajectories are found by solving the differential equation obtained from the condition that the product of their slopes at the intersection point is -1.

If the original family has slope m₁ = dy/dx,
then orthogonal trajectories have slope m₂ = -1/m₁

Method to Find Orthogonal Trajectories

Step 1

Start with the equation of the family of curves: F(x, y, c) = 0

Step 2

Differentiate to eliminate the constant c and obtain a differential equation: dy/dx = f(x, y)

Step 3

Replace dy/dx with -dx/dy to get the differential equation for orthogonal trajectories

Step 4

Solve the new differential equation to find the orthogonal trajectories

Real-World Applications

  • Electrostatics: Electric field lines and equipotential surfaces are orthogonal trajectories
  • Fluid Dynamics: Streamlines and equipotential lines in fluid flow
  • Heat Transfer: Isotherms and heat flow lines
  • Cartography: Mapping geographical coordinates
  • Optics: Light rays and wavefronts in geometric optics

Quick Example

For the family of circles centered at the origin: x² + y² = c²

Differentiating gives: 2x + 2y(dy/dx) = 0

So dy/dx = -x/y

For orthogonal trajectories: dy/dx = y/x

Solving gives: y = kx (lines through origin)

Thus, lines through the origin are orthogonal to circles centered at the origin.
